it's time to stop counting time

My work, my love

I work, and I empathise with everyone who also has to work. I'm not talking about those who do something they enjoy or who have found a profession that fits their personality. What I see in most of the jobs I've had, and in the experiences of the people I talk to, is that most people work out of obligation, not pleasure. Obligation to pay rent, food, water, electricity, and gas—obligations that make us go back to work day after day, without stopping, except for about 20 days of vacation a year. And yes, I feel it's an obligation. If I stop working, I have only a few months of freedom (depending on how much you can save per month). We fight every day for our freedom, but honestly, it's exhausting. It's exhausting to work 8 hours a day, 5 days a week. It's exhausting to think that I'll have to work the rest of my life just to survive.

There are 365 days in a year, but let's translate that into hours: there are 8,760 hours per year. If we subtract 8 hours for sleep every day (honestly, in my case it's never 8 hours, but counting 8, which is considered healthy, even knowing that for some people it's not enough), that's 2,920 hours less, leaving 5,840 hours. Of those hours, we have to do other things necessary for our survival: pay all the bills, go to the supermarket, commute to and from work (2 hours), cook (2 hours), take care of and educate children if applicable (3 hours), use the bathroom (1 hour). And I should include here as mandatory the time for socialising—something I consider essential for my own sanity—but let's leave those hours for later, as they are also not a priority in our European society (even if they are a higher priority than in the rest of the world or I do believe so). I will consider that for all these things we should have at least 8 hours a day, which gives 2,920 hours. Taking away what's left of the year's working hours, we're left with only 2,920 hours. Considering that, with the minimum wage, I need to work 8 hours a day, 5 days a week (in most European countries), and taking away the 21 days of vacation and the sacred weekends, we work about 2,187 hours a year. This leaves us with 733 free hours. Translating this back into days and adding vacation days and weekends to these 733 hours of free time, we get 30 days + 14 days (vacation without sleeping) + 64 days (weekends without sleeping), which gives 108 days of freedom—time to go where we want, do what we want, be with whomever we want.
